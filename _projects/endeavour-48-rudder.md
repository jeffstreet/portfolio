With the [hull shape established](http://jeffstreet.com/post/103809318347/endeavour48-v4-lines), it’s time to design the rudder. The original Endeavour’s rudder is in the aft edge of the keel. That is, the rudder was cut out of the keel and reattached with a hinge. We’ll do the same, and today’s task is to design the hinge line.

There isn’t much theory available to help us with proper rudder sizing and geometry for model yachts, so we’ll rely on logic and precedent.

Let’s start by thinking about the consequence of making the rudder too small. There are two forces at play: the rudder and the sail. If the rudder is too small, the sail will overpower the rudder and the boat would be unmanageable.

Fortunately, there’s little downside to making the rudder too large. The hull shape has been established and we are simply going to make it flexible without changing the area. Therefore, making the rudder larger does not add wetted area, which adds drag. In fact, large control surfaces do not have to be deflected as much as smaller control surfaces to generate the same force, and smaller deflections mean less drag.

These considerations tell us that it would be better to error on the side of making the rudder too large. So, we’ll look at the rudders used on similar boats, and make ours a bit larger. The image above shows our [Endeavour 48 - Version 4 lines](http://jeffstreet.com/post/103809318347/endeavour48-v4-lines), overlaid with out [Endeavour 48 - Version 1 lines](http://jeffstreet.com/post/103802593357/endeavour48-v1) and the [Endeavour full-scale lines](http://jeffstreet.com/post/103803587147/endeavour48-full-scale-lines).

We decided to cut our rudder along the blue lines, and reattach it to the hull with a hinge along the more vertical blue line. We chose this hinge line because it allows for increased rudder area while preserving a scale-like profile. This design also gives flexibility. It’s likely that the rudder design will be revised in the future. The rudder can be extended aft indefinitely, while still making a scale-looking joint with the keel and stern.
